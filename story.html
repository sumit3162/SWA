Our Story

Why Smart Ward Assistant exists

I am Sumit Shingne, an engineering student and innovator from the small village of Chincholi Shingne, Amravati, Maharashtra – 444705. Growing up in a rural area, I closely witnessed the real challenges faced by villages and small hospitals every single day. In cities, hospitals are equipped with advanced ICU systems, smart monitoring machines, and rapid emergency response systems. But in rural hospitals and Primary Health Centers (PHCs), the reality is very different.

In our villages, a single nurse is often responsible for an entire ward of patients. During the night shift, one nurse may handle emergency cases, injections, medicines, patient records, and sometimes even labor room emergencies alone. Continuous monitoring of every patient becomes almost impossible.

One real incident deeply changed my thinking. Late at night in a rural hospital, a patient suffering from breathing complications was admitted for observation. The patient’s oxygen level slowly started dropping, but there was no monitoring system available to detect the danger early. At the same time, the nurse was busy handling another emergency in a different room. Since there was no smart alert system, no one noticed the patient’s condition worsening for a long time.

By the time the problem was identified, the situation had already become critical. That moment made me realize something important: the problem was not the dedication of healthcare workers. The real problem was the lack of affordable monitoring infrastructure in rural healthcare systems.

In many villages, hospitals cannot afford ICU monitors costing ₹1–3 lakh for every bed. Internet connectivity is unstable, power cuts are common, and medical staff are limited. Because of this, many patients remain unmonitored between manual checkups.

I started asking myself a simple question: “Why should smart healthcare only exist in big cities?” That question became the beginning of the Smart Ward Assistant.

As an engineering student passionate about IoT, Embedded Systems, and Edge-AI, I began researching how technology could solve real rural healthcare problems at a low cost. I wanted to build something practical, affordable, simple to use, and independent from expensive cloud systems.

After months of research, testing, circuit designing, coding, and real-world problem analysis, the Smart Ward Assistant was born.

The system uses wearable sensors and ESP32-based Edge-AI technology to continuously monitor patient vitals like heart rate and oxygen levels. Instead of depending on internet-based cloud systems, it works completely offline using ESP-NOW communication technology.

The goal was simple:

  • Detect emergencies faster
  • Reduce manual workload on nurses
  • Improve patient safety
  • Support rural hospitals with affordable technology

One of the biggest innovations of the project is the voice alert system. Instead of only showing numbers on a screen or using confusing beep alarms, the system gives direct voice alerts such as: “Emergency detected on Bed Number 3.” This helps nurses respond immediately even while working on other tasks.

The Smart Ward Assistant was designed specifically for rural hospitals, PHCs, small clinics, emergency wards, health camps, and home healthcare.

What makes this project special is not just the technology — it is the purpose behind it. This project was not created inside a luxury lab or backed by huge funding. It was inspired by real village healthcare struggles, real patients, real nurses, and real emergencies that happen silently every day in rural India.

Today, our mission is to bridge the healthcare gap between villages and cities by creating affordable smart healthcare solutions that anyone can access.

We believe healthcare innovation should not depend on location, internet, or hospital budget. Every patient deserves safety. Every nurse deserves support. Every rural hospital deserves smart healthcare technology.

Smart Ward Assistant is more than a project. It is a step toward safer and smarter rural healthcare for India.
